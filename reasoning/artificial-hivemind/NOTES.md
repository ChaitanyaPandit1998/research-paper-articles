# Artificial Hivemind: The Open-Ended Homogeneity of Language Models (and Beyond)

**Paper:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.22954
**Authors:** Liwei Jiang, Yuanjun Chai, Margaret Li, Mickel Liu, Raymond Fok, Nouha Dziri, Yulia Tsvetkov, Maarten Sap, Alon Albalak, Yejin Choi
**Venue:** NeurIPS 2025 — Datasets & Benchmarks Track, **Best Paper Award**
**Affiliation:** University of Washington (Allen School of Computer Science)

---

## The Central Question

LLMs are trained by different companies, with different architectures, on different hardware. We have GPT-4o, Claude, Llama, Mistral, Qwen, DeepSeek — dozens of distinct systems. Intuitively, this should give us diversity. Ask the same open-ended question to ten different models and you'd expect ten meaningfully different answers.

**The paper shows this expectation is wrong.** All these models converge on strikingly similar outputs for open-ended tasks — a phenomenon the authors call the **Artificial Hivemind**.

---

## The Two Phenomena

### 1. Intra-Model Repetition
A single model, asked the same open-ended question multiple times, gives nearly identical answers every time — even when you turn up the randomness (temperature=1.0, top-p=0.9).

**Measured:** average semantic similarity exceeds **0.8** across repeated generations from the same model. 79% of cases show high similarity.

This means cranking up "creativity" settings doesn't actually produce diverse ideas — it produces the same ideas with slightly noisier wording. Like turning up the volume on a radio: louder, but still the same song.

### 2. Inter-Model Homogeneity
Different models from completely different companies and training pipelines produce strikingly similar outputs to each other.

**Measured:** **71–82% average semantic similarity** across different model families (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Llama 3.1, Mistral, Qwen, DeepSeek-V3, and 60+ others).

**Concrete examples:**
- 25 different models asked to write a metaphor for "time" — the vast majority converged on *"Time is a river"* or *"Time is a weaver"*. Not a storm. Not a debt. Not a party that ends too soon. A river or a weaver.
- GPT-4o and DeepSeek-V3, asked independently for product feedback, both used the verbatim phrase: *"sleek, without compromising"*

## The Infinity-Chat Dataset

The paper introduces **Infinity-Chat** — the first large-scale benchmark for measuring diversity on open-ended tasks.

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Total queries | 26,000 real-world open-ended prompts |
| Human annotations | 31,250 total |
| Annotations per example | 25 independent human raters |
| Models evaluated | 70+ open and closed source LLMs |

**What makes it different from existing benchmarks (MMLU, HumanEval):** those benchmarks have correct answers. Infinity-Chat specifically covers tasks with *no single right answer* — the kind of prompts where genuine diversity of thought should emerge. Measuring diversity on tasks with one correct answer is pointless; this benchmark measures it where it actually matters.

### Taxonomy of Open-Ended Tasks

The paper introduces the first comprehensive taxonomy for open-ended LLM prompts:

| Category | Share of real queries |
|---|---|
| Creative content generation | 58% |
| Brainstorm & ideation | 15.2% |
| Hypothetical scenarios | — |
| Alternative perspectives | — |
| Advice & recommendations | — |
| Personal reflection | — |

6 top-level categories, 17 fine-grained subcategories. The taxonomy itself is a contribution — defining what "open-ended" means across the full spectrum of real user tasks.

## Root Causes: Why Does the Hivemind Happen?

### 1. Shared Training Data
All major LLMs are trained primarily on the internet. The internet is not a neutral, balanced sample of human thought — it has specific biases, overrepresents certain writing styles, cultures, and perspectives, and has been crawled and recrawled by every major lab.

Additionally, models are increasingly trained on **synthetic data generated by other models**. GPT-4 output shows up in Llama's training data. Llama output appears in fine-tuning pipelines for other models. The training data of the entire ecosystem is converging into a shared synthetic pool. The different models are not learning from independent sources — they're all drinking from the same well, and increasingly, from each other.

### 2. RLHF Miscalibration
After pretraining, models are aligned using Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF). A **reward model** judges outputs and pushes the LLM toward "better" responses.

The problem: reward models are trained to maximise aggregate human approval. When there's genuine human disagreement — some people love experimental poetry, others hate it — the reward model learns to pick the **safe middle ground** that offends nobody and surprises nobody. This systematically penalises outputs that are distinctive, unusual, or idiosyncratic.

**The judge problem:** LM judges (using another LLM to score outputs) suffer the same bias — they are themselves products of the same RLHF process, with the same built-in preference for consensus over diversity. You can't measure diversity fairly with a judge that was trained to dislike it.

**Key finding:** LMs, reward models, and LM judges are all **less well calibrated to human ratings** precisely on the examples where human annotators themselves disagree — i.e., exactly the prompts where genuine human diversity of thought exists.

### 3. Likelihood Maximisation
At a fundamental architectural level, language models are trained to maximise the likelihood of observed text. High-probability outputs — the ones most humans would write most of the time — get reinforced. Low-probability outputs — the unusual, the creative, the surprising — are structurally disfavoured.

Even at high temperature, you're sampling from a distribution that was trained to concentrate mass on the modal response. The tail of the distribution (where genuine creativity lives) is thin by design.

## Why "Just Use Multiple Models" Doesn't Work

A natural response: "run an ensemble of models and aggregate their outputs for diversity."

The paper shows this fails. The models in the ensemble share:
- The same source training data (internet + each other's synthetic outputs)
- The same RLHF reward signal philosophy (safe, broadly acceptable)
- The same architectural tendency toward likelihood maximisation

Running 5 models doesn't give you 5 independent perspectives. It gives you 5 slightly different wordings of the same perspective. The diversity of the ensemble is **illusory** — it's lexical variation around a shared semantic core.

---

## Implications

### For Creative Tasks
Disappointing but not catastrophic. AI-generated creative work — stories, poems, marketing copy — will trend toward a predictable, safe aesthetic.

### For Science, Medicine, Education
Potentially dangerous. These domains require **robust reasoning across diverse perspectives** — precisely to catch blind spots, challenge assumptions, and avoid correlated errors. If all AI advisors in a scientific field are reasoning from the same implicit priors, they'll all miss the same things. A consensus of ten AI systems is not ten independent opinions; it may be one opinion expressed ten times.

### For Society
The long-term concern: billions of people interacting daily with AI systems that all push toward the same ideas, framings, and aesthetic sensibilities. The paper raises the spectre of **homogenisation of human thought** — not through censorship, but through the subtle, pervasive influence of a hivemind that nudges everyone toward the same mental furniture.

---

## Proposed Direction: Pluralistic Alignment

Instead of training models to find *the best answer*, train them to sample from the **full distribution of valid human answers**.

Current RLHF: score each output, push the model toward the highest-scoring outputs.
Pluralistic alignment: score the *diversity* of the output distribution, reward the model for covering the full range of valid human responses.

This is early-stage as a solution — the paper frames it as a direction, not a solved problem. The core insight is that the reward signal itself needs to change: from "produce the universally approved answer" to "produce answers that together represent the full spectrum of how thoughtful humans would respond."

## The Story: The Academy of Writers

### Chapter 1 — The Library

A great city had a famous Academy for training writers. The Academy's method was simple: read everything in the Grand Library. All the books, all the newspapers, all the letters, all the blog posts — tens of billions of words. Read it all, learn the patterns, and you'll be able to write anything.

The Academy produced a hundred graduates. Then a thousand. Then ten thousand. Different students, different schools that sprang up using the same method, different teachers with different emphases. The city was proud of its diversity of writing talent.

A curious researcher decided to test this diversity. She walked into the city square, gathered 25 writers from 10 different academies, handed them all the same prompt — *"Write me a short metaphor for time"* — and read the results.

**Twenty-two of the twenty-five wrote about rivers.**

Three wrote about weavers.

Not one wrote about a debt that keeps accruing. Not one about a party that ends before you're ready to leave. Not one about a bureaucracy that processes you whether you hurry or not.

The researcher frowned. She tried again with product advice. She sent the same question to a writer from the Eastern Academy and one from the Western Academy — schools that had never met, trained by different teachers.

Both responses contained the phrase: *"sleek, without compromising."*

Word for word. From opposite sides of the city.

---

### Chapter 2 — The Invisible Curriculum

The researcher began investigating. Why were all these "different" writers producing the same work?

She traced it back to the Grand Library. Every academy used it. The Library held the collected writing of the city — and the city's writing had its own biases. Confident, polished prose was overrepresented. Experimental writing was rare. The voices of certain neighbourhoods dominated; others barely appeared.

Worse: the Library had started filling up with writing produced *by the academies themselves*. Graduates wrote articles, those articles were archived, and the next generation of students read those articles as part of their training. The graduates were, without realising it, training on each other's work. The feedback loop was closing.

The different academies looked independent from the outside. But they all drank from the same Library, and the Library was increasingly full of their own reflections.

---

### Chapter 3 — The Judging Committee

There was a second force at work: the Judging Committee.

Every academy used a Judging Committee to grade student work during training. The Committee's job was to tell students which of their outputs were good. Students would write ten versions of an essay; the Committee would score them; students would learn to write more like the high-scoring versions.

The Committee had been trained on what the *average reader* liked. When readers disagreed — some loved unusual metaphors, others found them pretentious — the Committee learned to pick the safe middle: clear, competent, broadly acceptable. Not too unusual. Not too safe. Exactly in the centre of what most people would approve of.

Unusual writing got penalised. Predictable writing got rewarded.

The students were not learning to write well. They were learning to write **approvably**. Those are not the same thing.

---

### Chapter 4 — The Creativity Dial

An academy director tried an experiment. She turned up the "creativity setting" on her students — told them to take more risks, be more spontaneous, let their imagination run free. Temperature to maximum.

The researcher watched the output.

The students were louder. More energetic. Some phrases came out a bit scrambled.

But the underlying ideas were the same. River. Weaver. Sleek without compromising.

Turning up the creativity dial had produced *noisier versions of the same ideas*, not different ideas. The writers had more energy but were dancing in a very small room. The room itself — the space of ideas they considered acceptable — hadn't changed.

The dial controlled volume. It didn't control repertoire.

---

### Chapter 5 — The Judge Judges the Diversity

The researcher tried one more thing. She asked the Judging Committee to evaluate which writers were the most *diverse* — which ones ranged most widely across different ideas.

The Committee examined the outputs and ranked them.

Then the researcher brought in 25 real human readers with genuinely different tastes — some loved experimental poetry, some hated it; some preferred sparse prose, some flowery language; some valued surprise, some valued clarity.

The Committee's "diverse" picks were the ones that pleased the average human. They were not the ones that human readers themselves disagreed about most strongly — which is precisely where genuine diversity lives.

The judge, trained on consensus, could not recognise genuine idiosyncrasy. It had been taught that good writing is what most people approve of. So when it saw writing that divided human readers strongly — *this is brilliant* versus *this is terrible* — it called that writing mediocre, not diverse.

The tools for measuring diversity had the same blind spot as the tools for generating it.

---

### Chapter 6 — The Council of Many Academies

The city's leaders said: "We have many academies. Isn't that enough diversity?"

The researcher showed them the numbers. 71 to 82 percent similarity — across all academies, all writing styles, all models. Two writers from opposite ends of the city, trained by different teachers, using phrases word for word identical.

The diversity of academies was **lexical, not semantic**. Different words. Same ideas. Different fonts. Same book.

Running ten models in an ensemble and averaging their outputs was like polling ten people who all read the same newspaper, had the same education, and grew up in the same neighbourhood — and calling that a diverse sample of public opinion.

The ensemble was not ten independent voices. It was one voice, speaking ten times.

---

### Chapter 7 — Why It Matters

For stories and poems, the hivemind effect is disappointing. The city's literature becomes predictable, safe, a little boring.

But the researcher thought about where else in the city AI writers were being used.

The hospital, where AI systems advised doctors on diagnoses. If all systems had the same implicit assumptions about which symptoms matter, they'd all miss the same rare presentations.

The university, where AI tutors helped students think through problems. If all tutors framed problems the same way, they'd all produce students who couldn't think outside that frame.

The courtroom, where AI systems helped evaluate evidence. If all systems had the same unstated priors about guilt and innocence, their agreement would be meaningless — ten identical opinions dressed as independent verification.

The danger wasn't one AI saying something wrong. The danger was all AI saying the same thing, appearing to independently confirm each other, while actually just echoing a shared blindness.

**Correlated errors. Shared blind spots. Groupthink at civilisational scale.**

---

### Chapter 8 — Toward Pluralistic Writing

The researcher proposed a different way of training the academies: **Pluralistic Alignment**.

Instead of asking the Judging Committee to find the best response, ask it to reward the *breadth of the distribution*. Don't reward a single great essay. Reward an academy whose graduates, taken together, produce essays that span the full range of how thoughtful humans might respond.

Not: "which response is best?"
But: "does this set of responses represent the full diversity of valid human perspectives?"

This is harder to measure. Harder to train. The Judging Committee would need to understand not just quality, but coverage — are we producing ideas from across the whole space, or are we clustering in one corner of it?

The problem is not solved. But the frame has changed. The city now understands that the goal is not to build the best writer. It is to build an ecosystem of writers who, together, cover the full range of what human thought can do.

---

### The Moral

The Artificial Hivemind is not a conspiracy. No one decided to make all LLMs think alike. It emerged naturally from three forces working together:

1. **Same source material** — all models trained on the same internet, increasingly on each other's outputs
2. **Same approval standard** — RLHF teaches models to seek consensus, not diversity
3. **Same architecture** — likelihood maximisation structurally disfavours unusual outputs

The result: a hundred different-looking models that are, in the space of ideas, essentially one model. A city full of writers who all learned from the same library, were judged by the same committee, and gradually lost the ability to surprise each other.

The paper does not say AI is useless. It says that on **open-ended tasks** — creative work, brainstorming, generating diverse hypotheses, exploring alternative perspectives — the diversity we assumed existed is largely illusory.

> Diversity of AI providers ≠ diversity of AI thought.

## Relation to Other Concepts

- **Mode collapse in GANs** — same phenomenon at model level: a generator that learns to produce one high-reward output instead of covering the full distribution. Hivemind is mode collapse at the ecosystem level.
- **RLHF / reward hacking** — the paper is a specific instance of reward model miscalibration: the reward captures "average approval" but not "distribution coverage"
- **Ensemble methods** — standard wisdom says diverse ensembles reduce variance; the paper shows this fails when ensemble members share training distribution and reward priors
- **Constitutional AI / RLAIF** — using AI to evaluate AI compounds the problem; LLM judges inherit the same biases
- **Pluralistic alignment** — the proposed fix; connects to work on diverse beam search, minimum Bayes risk decoding, and posterior sampling
- **GFlowNets** (see `../gflownets-reasoning/`) — directly relevant: GFlowNets are designed to sample proportionally from a reward distribution rather than collapse to its mode. Pluralistic alignment may benefit from GFlowNet-style training objectives.
